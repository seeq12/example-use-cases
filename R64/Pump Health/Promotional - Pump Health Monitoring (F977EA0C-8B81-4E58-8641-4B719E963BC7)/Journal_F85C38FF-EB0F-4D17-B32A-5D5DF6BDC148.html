Pump Health Monitoring

Link to Organizer Topic

Summary

Pumps are a critical asset for movement of liquid streams in manufacturing facilities across all industry verticals. Keeping pumps healthy and operational helps keep processes up and running at target throughput rates. Inability to detect and anticipate pump performance issues can lead to prolonged shutdown, loss of revenue, and environmental or safety threats.

This use case seeks to identify leading and lagging indicators of pump health and continuously monitor these indicator variables to detect poor performance and take corrective actions.

Step 1. Pull in Relevant Pump Monitoring Data

Some signals that can be used to calculate KPIS providing insight into overall pump health might include: flow rates, discharge and suction pressures, suction temperature, upstream tank level (static head), vibration data, and pump status signals. 

Additional contextual data that can be helpful in evaluating pump performance might include: pump curve, pump data sheet, liquid properties, and failure or maintenance event data. 

(full work step)

 

Step 2. Cleanse the data used in calculations 

Signal filtering can be applied to remove noise from signals. Outliers and downtime data should be removed from signals when calculating monitoring limits. Interpolation settings can be adjusted to leave gaps in the data during long outages and linearly interpolate through point outliers. Signals can be repaired to read zero when an unreasonable negative flow reading occurs. (full work step)

  • Identify downtimes or periods when the pump is not operating
  • Remove downtime data, outliers, smooth signals

  • Convert liquid property data, like API to SG 

 

Step 3. Compare Pump Operation to Design 

Data from the pump curve and design spec sheet can be incorporated into Seeq enabling us to compare our cleansed pump data to design data. (full work step)

  • Total Flow through the pump (discharge + recirculation)
  • Design Discharge Pressure (calculated from total flow + pump curve data)
  • +/-30% boundary limits for design discharge pressure
  • Identify when pump is operating outside of design window

Step 4. Pump Operation Mode Comparison

With pump curve/design operation incorporated into Seeq, we can begin to define other operating modes based on deviation from the Best Efficiency Point (B.E.P. on image below).

We can define conditions for each of these unique operating modes (work step), then combine them into a comprehensive Histogram view. (full work step)

Based on pump curve, the best operating point is at flowrate of 290 GPM (9942.86 bbl/day)which corresponds with 8100 ft .  Normal operating mode  is usually around ±10% BEP flowrate (8948  to 10937 bbl/day) . Other operating modes are identified based on how far the pump is being operating at from BEP. 

  • High Temp Rise (less than 3977bbl/d)
  • Low Flow Cavitation (3977 to 4971 bbl/d)
  • Low bearing/seal life (low flow) (4971 to 5966 bbl/d)
  • Reduced impeller life (5966 to 6960 bbl/d)
  • Suction recirculation (6960 to7954 bbl/d)
  • Discharge recirculation ( 7954 to 8948 bbl/d)
  • Low bearing/seal life (high flow) (10937 to 11931 bbl/d)
  • High flow cavitation  (higher than 11931 bbl/d)
  • Cavitation due to low NPSH
  • Normal operation (8948  to 10937 bbl/day)
  • Combined Histogram view

 

Step 5. Pump Daily Health Score Calculation

This histogram created in step 4 provides a visual comparison of time spent in different operating modes, but a more useful representation of the data combines the parameters of interest into a single "Daily Health Score" that represents the overall health of the pump. This value can be monitored for deviations from a healthy range, established during the early life of the pump when performance is ideal and efficiency is high. (full work step)
