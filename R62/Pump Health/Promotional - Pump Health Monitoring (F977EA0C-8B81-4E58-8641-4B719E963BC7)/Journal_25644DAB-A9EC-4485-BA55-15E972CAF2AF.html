Pump Operation Mode Identification - Overview

Pumps are considered as critical equipment for any processing units which its failure can cause prolonged shutdowns. Pump operation optimization can lead to significant cost saving and revenue generation. 

The premise of the Demo is:

  1. How to identify pump operation modes in a capsule format
  2. How to calculate the total count of time for each pump operation modes in a given time range

Based on pump curve, the best operating point is at flowrate of 290 GPM (9942.86 bbl/day)which corresponds with 8100 ft .  Normal operating mode  is usually around ±10% BEP flowrate (8948  to 10937 bbl/day) . Other operating mode can be identified based on how far the pump is being operating at from BEP. 

Calculation Steps

Step 1: Input Signal

  • Total flow
  • Discharge pressure
  • NPSH Available (based on pump DS)

Step 2: Calculated NPSH required based on pump curve and liquid SG. (Step) 

Step 3: Define the following operating mode based on the deviation from the best efficiency point (workstep)

  • High Temp Rise (less than 3977bbl/d)
  • Low Flow Cavitation (3977 to 4971 bbl/d)
  • Low bearing/seal life (low flow) (4971 to 5966 bbl/d)
  • Reduced impeller life (5966 to 6960 bbl/d)
  • Suction recirculation (6960 to7954 bbl/d)
  • Discharge recirculation ( 7954 to 8948 bbl/d)
  • Low bearing/seal life (high flow) (10937 to 11931 bbl/d)
  • High flow cavitation  (higher than 11931 bbl/d)
  • Cavitation due to low NPSH
  • Normal operation (8948  to 10937 bbl/day)
  • High Suction Temperature (>600 F for 1 hour)
  • Low Drum Level (<20% for 1 hour)

Step 4: Combine all conditions except for Cavitation due to low NPSH (Workstep)

Step 5. Use Histogram to calculate total time at each operational mode for a given time range (workstep)
